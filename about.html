introduction

Hi — I'm Emma, a sophomore studying Computer Engineering at Georgia Tech with a 3.71 GPA. I'm from Decatur, Georgia, where I grew up taking things apart to figure out how they worked. That instinct is still very much alive: today it just looks like reverse-engineering malware in IDA Pro or designing custom PCBs for competition robots.

This site is my professional portfolio — a place to document what I've built, where I'm headed, and what I care about. Whether you're a recruiter, a collaborator, or just curious, welcome.

background

I grew up in Decatur, GA, where I was a competitive musician (principal clarinet, All-State Band) and a STEM obsessive in equal measure — captaining my school's Science Olympiad team and spending evenings on our FIRST Robotics team, where we designed and built 150+ lb robots from scratch in under six weeks. Those years taught me how to work under pressure, mentor others, and ship hardware on a deadline.

At Georgia Tech, my interests have crystallized around two things: the security of systems — how they break, how they're exploited, how they're defended — and the craft of building good software. I'm part of RoboJackets Robowrestling (3rd place, 500g competition) and Greyhat, GT's cybersecurity club, where I've been deepening my reverse engineering work.

what I work on

My projects span a wide range — from a hackathon full-stack pantry web app with an AI recipe generator, to line-by-line reverse engineering of real-world malware like W32.SQLSlammer and Michelangelo, to a real-time 2D game running on an ARM Mbed microcontroller. I like working close to the metal as much as I like building user-facing products.

On the security side, I'm particularly interested in binary analysis, malware behavior, and exploit mechanics. I find the puzzle of reconstructing what code is actually doing in IDA Pro — no source, no symbols, just assembly — genuinely satisfying.

outside of engineering

I'm a musician first and a hardware hacker second — or maybe the other way around depending on the week. I play bass in a band, have been playing instruments since I was a kid, and find that music and engineering scratch the same itch: both reward precision, pattern recognition, and knowing when to break the rules.

I'm also trilingual (English, Mandarin, French) and enjoy the mental context-switch of moving between languages — not unlike switching between a Python backend and x86 assembly.

And yes, those are my cats in the photo — Kiwi and Cloud. They are extremely rambunctious and entirely unhelpful when I'm trying to debug at 2am.
